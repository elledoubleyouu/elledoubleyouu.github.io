say something real
Most of the words people say on any given average day would have been better off unsaid. Most of what we say mean nothing at all. They're words, sentences, paragraphs to fill the air, the silence between us. I care nothing for your "how was your weekend?" and "how are you?"s. What if we were each allocated a limited number of words a day? Would the scarcity make us think twice before we opened our mouths? We would save our words for the right moments, the right people. Maybe the only words we're allowed to use are the words we would put into a theme song or a poem. And we would be able to find peace even as we drown in a sea of people.

That sounds like a utopia to me.

It reminds me of that scene in Lost in Translation, when Bill Murray is walking down a Tokyo street, his head floating above the crowd.

What might be the worst is when, at the end of the day, all you're looking for is to hear, feel something real, maybe one soundbite that means something, anything at all—but you wade through these words upon words like the colorful balls in a bouncy house and there isn't one that holds more weight than cheap plastic. That's the worst.
Set in Monoton and Assistant.
